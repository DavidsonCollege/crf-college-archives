Open Forum

YES, A BURNING SHAME.

Read the Exchanges in the “Y” Room. (An article came forth in the Open Forum under the heading “A Burning
Shame” and under the signature “Chemical,” last week with which I wish to take strenuous issue. I grant that it
was a burning shame; a burning shame that the Forum Editor should have let such an article get into the
paper; a burning shame that even granting all the article claims to be true, which we do not, that we
should have on the campus a man who waits till a man is down and then hits him—a man who
doesn't possess the first instincts of sportsmanship; a burning shame that the Davidsonian had allowed such an article to go
far and wide, which has in it a few kernels of truth, but which for the most part grows out
of the fertile imagination of Chemical.

He starts off by harping on our “very short-sighted policy” in regard to fire-fighting apparatus. We grant that we could
and should have had better fighting apparatus—for we certainly have learned our lesson through experience. And yet it was not
the fault of the college authorities that there was not sufficient pressure in the water mains that night to throw
a stream to the top of Chambers building. The drought and the scarcity of water all over this part of
the country was the main cause for this. Just this year the college has spent considerable money to install pumps
which were guaranteed to keep the tank full—but what can man do against nature, the pumps cannot pump unless there
is water to be pumped. Perhaps Chemical, with his sparkling brilliance, might tell us how to get water out of
wells that, on account of the drought, have only a limited supply. The chemical machine which was purchased this fall
would have been enough to put the fire out if it had reached the spot on time, but after the
flames had made the headway they had, an up-to-date city apparatus would have had a difficult time overcoming them. Yes,
we needed a more elaborate fire-fighting apparatus, but it is doubtful whether that would have saved the building.

And then, the writer makes this statement, “Our growth has been a superficial one of late.” He brings up the
fact that to all appearances the only growth has been in quantity, and that the quality had been ignored. He
insinuates that the Freshmen this year are not as well prepared as those of former years, and yet I have
a statement from the president of the college that this year’s class is the best-prepared class that ever entered Davidson,
that with the exception of three or four men who wanted special work before taking up the ministry, every man
in the class presented his 15 credits as entrance requirements. Once more, Chemical spoke whereof he did not know.

What’s more, the writer insinuates that the increase in numbers is due entirely to a desire on the part of
the faculty and trustees to have more men in the college for the mere purpose of greater quantity. He seems
to forget entirely that the pressure from outside has been enormous, that the demands of the young men of the
Church for a college naturally being loyal Presbyterians, they want to come to Davidson. With the increase in numbers has
come an increase in prices. It was impossible to maintain the same prices, and the result was the barracks. They
are not ideal, but they were put in for an emergency and they have served their purpose.

There are many of us who think that the number should be limited to 500, that the present number is
sufficient. But there are also those who want the college to continue to grow and grow until it develops into
a large Southern Presbyterian University. We all have our personal opinions, but we should also have the utmost respect for
those who differ from us.

IRREPARABLE LOSS.

“Anybody hurt in the wreck?” “One gentleman, I believe.” “Bones broken?” “I think it was his heart. He sat down
by a leaking suitcase and shed tears.” —Ex.

A great opportunity for Tech men was opened when the convocation in control of the Rhodes Scholarship at Oxford decided
that a knowledge of the Greek language would not be considered a requisite to obtain the scholarship, a decision which
sums up the growing twentieth-century interest in the practical and in present-day affairs. It makes possible the achievement by Tech
men of the highest honor which may be accorded to an American student. —S. I. N. A.
